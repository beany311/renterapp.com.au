How to write a rental cover letter that actually gets you noticed

Updated June 2026 · 8 min read

The Australian rental market in 2026 is brutal. Vacancy rates in Melbourne and Sydney are sitting near record lows, and the average rental property in most inner suburbs attracts between 30 and 80 applications. Most of those applications look identical: same income, same references, same 100-point ID check. A cover letter is often the only place you can actually sound like a person.

Most renters skip this step entirely. They fill in the form, attach their payslips, and hope for the best. That means if you write a short, genuine, well-targeted cover letter, you are already ahead of the majority of people applying for the same property.

Why a rental cover letter matters

Property managers at busy agencies can receive 50 or more applications for a single listing within 24 hours. When they sit down to sort through them, the financials do the first round of filtering: income, employment, rental history. But after that? Most applicants look the same on paper.

A cover letter gives you a few sentences to tip the scale. It tells the agent who you actually are, why you want this specific property, and why you would be a reliable tenant. Agents have said publicly that a personalised, well-written letter can move an applicant from the middle of the pile to the shortlist, especially when the financial profiles are similar.

It also signals effort. An agent who sees 60 copy-pasted applications and one that clearly references the property and the neighbourhood is going to notice the one that stands out.

What to include in your rental cover letter

Keep it under 200 words. Agents are busy and do not want to read an essay. Here is what to cover, in roughly this order:

Your name and a brief intro

One sentence. Who you are and who will be living there. No need for a formal salutation if you do not know the agent's name.

Why this specific property

This is the most important part and the one most people skip. Mention something specific: the proximity to your workplace, the school catchment, the layout, the outdoor space. Generic phrases like "I love the location" do not count. If you walked through the inspection, mention something you noticed. Agents can tell immediately whether you mean it.

Your employment and income

One or two sentences. Where you work, what you do, how long you have been there, and whether you are permanent or on a contract. If your income is irregular (freelance, casual, self-employed), address it head-on rather than leaving it to the documents to explain.

Your rental history

Briefly mention how long you have been renting, whether you have had any disputes, and that you can provide references from previous property managers. If this is your first rental, say so honestly and lead with your stability in other areas (job, savings, references from employers or teachers).

Who will be living there

Be upfront. Mention the number of adults and children, whether anyone works from home, and any other relevant details about how the property will be used.

Lease length preference

If you are open to a longer lease, say so. Landlords often prefer the certainty of a 24-month lease over a 12-month one, and signalling this can work in your favour.

Pets

If you have a pet, do not hide it. Disclose it upfront, mention the breed and size, and describe how the pet is managed (indoor/outdoor, training, previous rental history with pets). Trying to sneak a pet into an application almost never ends well.

A closing line

Something simple. That you are available for a reference call, that you can be flexible on the start date, or just that you would be grateful for the opportunity. Keep it warm, not desperate.

Keep in mind

A cover letter is a supplement to your application, not a replacement for strong documentation. You still need to provide payslips, ID, rental history, and references. The letter just makes the human case.

What agents actually look for

Beyond the cover letter itself, understanding what agents are evaluating helps you write a better one. Here is what consistently comes up when agents talk about what separates strong applications from weak ones:

  • Income to rent ratio. A widely used benchmark is that your gross annual income should be at least three times the annual rent. So for a property at $500 per week ($26,000 per year), agents typically want to see household income of at least $78,000. If you are borderline, acknowledge it and explain why your situation is stable despite the number.
  • Stable employment. Permanent employment reads better than casual or contract work, even at the same income. If you are on a fixed-term contract, mention your employment history and whether your contract is likely to be renewed.
  • A clean rental history. No disputes, no tribunal hearings, no unpaid rent. If you have a blemish on your record, it is usually better to address it briefly than to leave it for the agent to discover.
  • Contactable references. Former property managers, not just personal friends. If you only have personal references, explain why and offer multiple options.
  • Personalisation. As noted above, agents notice when a letter was written for this property rather than copied from a template.
  • Willingness to sign a longer lease. A two-year lease is often more attractive to a landlord than a 12-month one. If you are genuinely planning to stay, mention it.

On the question of offering above the asking rent: it does happen in competitive markets, and in some cases it does influence the outcome. That is a separate decision from the cover letter itself, but worth knowing about.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Using a generic template. Agents see hundreds of applications. A letter that could apply to any property is immediately obvious and carries no weight.
  • Writing too much. Three to four short paragraphs is the right length. If your letter is running past 250 words, cut it.
  • Not mentioning the specific property. If you do not reference the address or something specific about the property, the letter reads as a mass application.
  • Leaving out your contact details. Include your phone number and email, even if they are already in the form. Make it easy for the agent to reach you.
  • Being too formal. "To whom it may concern, I write to express my sincere interest..." reads as stiff and impersonal. Agents are people. Write like one.
  • Being too casual or oversharing. On the other end, do not use slang, and do not include information that has no bearing on your tenancy. Keep it professional but warm.

Rental cover letter example

Here is a real-feeling example for a 2-bedroom apartment in Melbourne. Use it as a reference for tone and structure, not as a template to copy word for word.

Example cover letter

Hi, my name is Priya, and I am applying for the 2-bedroom apartment at 14 Clarence Street, Fitzroy, with my partner James.

We both work in the area and have been looking in Fitzroy for a while. When we inspected the property last Saturday, the north-facing living room and the proximity to the Edinburgh Gardens were exactly what we had been looking for. We are hoping to put down proper roots in the neighbourhood.

I am a registered nurse at the Royal Melbourne Hospital (permanent full-time, four years in this role) and James works as a software developer at a company in the CBD (permanent, three years). Our combined annual income is around $185,000.

We have rented together for three years in Carlton with no disputes. Our current property manager, Gemma at Harcourts Carlton, is happy to be contacted as a reference. We are also happy to sign a 24-month lease if that works better for the landlord.

We do not have any pets. We are available to move in from 1 July, with some flexibility if needed.

Thank you for considering our application. We would love to make this our home.

Priya Sharma · 0412 345 678 · priya.sharma@email.com

Notice what this letter does: it names the property, mentions something specific from the inspection, covers both applicants' employment clearly, references rental history, and addresses lease length and pets proactively. It is under 200 words and reads like a person wrote it.

Tips for different situations

Couples

Introduce both people briefly. Mention both incomes and both employment situations. If you have been renting together previously, reference that history.

Families with kids

Be upfront about children and ages. Agents appreciate honesty. Mention that you are looking for long-term stability, as families typically make lower-maintenance tenants.

Pet owners

Disclose everything: breed, size, desexed status, inside or outside. Offer to pay a higher bond if allowed by state law, or to have the carpets professionally cleaned at the end of the lease.

Students

Lead with a guarantor if you have one. Mention your course, institution, and expected graduation date. If parents are guaranteeing the lease, include their details.

Self-employed

Acknowledge the irregular income directly. Mention how long you have been operating, and reference your tax returns or accountant's letter as evidence of stability. Savings as a buffer can also be worth mentioning.

First-time renters

If you have no rental history, say so clearly rather than leaving a gap. Lean on employment references, savings, and a willingness to offer a longer lease to compensate.

State-specific tips

Victoria

Victorian rental law changed significantly in 2021 and again in 2023. Landlords and agents in Victoria cannot charge a fee for a rental application or require a holding deposit before a lease is signed. The bond is capped at one month's rent for most properties. If you are in a dispute, Consumer Affairs Victoria and VCAT are the main channels. It is worth mentioning in your cover letter if you are familiar with the process, as it signals you are an informed tenant rather than an uninformed one.

New South Wales

NSW is one of the more competitive markets, particularly inner Sydney. Rental applications go through various portals and agents often process them quickly. Reference checks are taken seriously here. Make sure your references are warned in advance that they may receive a call. NSW Fair Trading handles disputes and tenancy information.

Queensland

Queensland law allows landlords to request a "rental ledger" showing your payment history with a previous property manager. If yours is clean, mention it proactively and offer to provide it. The Residential Tenancies Authority (RTA) in Queensland also provides a free dispute resolution service, which is worth knowing if you ever need it.
